Mount Kilauea, Hawaii

By: Wakangari Olana, 6th Grade Science Project

Geographic Location

Mount Kilauea is on the Big Island of Hawaii inside Hawaii Volcanoes National Park. It’s in the southeastern part of the island near the Pacific Ocean. The coordinates are around 19.4°N, 155.3°W. It’s surrounded by lava fields and rainforests.

Geologic Situation

Kilauea is a shield volcano made from thin, runny lava that spreads out and builds wide, gentle slopes. It formed because the Pacific Plate is moving over a hotspot deep under the Earth. Unlike most volcanoes, it’s not at a plate boundary — the magma just rises straight up through the crust!

Event Data

One of the biggest eruptions was on May 3, 2018. Lava began flowing in the Puna District and lasted for months. Over 700 homes were destroyed, and new lava flows reached the ocean. The eruption officially ended in August 2018, but Kilauea still erupts sometimes today!

Effect on Earth / Environment

The eruption changed the land a lot. Lava flows covered forests, roads, and even made new land along the ocean. The air got filled with volcanic smog (called vog) that made breathing hard for some people and animals. Sulfur dioxide gas also made acid rain. But it also created new rock and land that scientists can study!

Effect on Humans / Civilization

Over 2,000 people had to evacuate their homes in 2018. Many lost their houses, farms, and roads. The eruption caused over $800 million in damages. But it also helped scientists learn how volcanoes behave and brought attention from tourists all over the world once it was safe again.
